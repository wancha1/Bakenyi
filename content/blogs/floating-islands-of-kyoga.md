To a visitor, the floating islands of **Lake Kyoga** look like a geographical novelty. To a Bakenyi fisherman, they are *Ebiswa* — a dynamic living workspace, a moving shield, and a marker of seasonal time.

These islands, composed of dense mats of papyrus roots, peat, and soil, can range from a few square meters to several square kilometers in size. They drift across the lake surface, driven by powerful equatorial winds and seasonal river currents.

### The Physics of Drift
The Bakenyi have mastered the art of "reading" the drift. By observing the tilt of reeds on the horizon and the scent of water spray, a fisherman can predict where a massive island will move several hours before it blocks a vital channel. This predictive science has been passed down orally from grandfather to grandson.

### Ecological Sanctuaries
*Ebiswa* are not just floating dirt; they are rich bio-shelters. They act as nurseries for young Tilapia and Nile Perch, protecting them from predatory birds. The root networks act as natural water filters, trapping sediments and ensuring healthy nutrient loops.

Understanding the *Ebiswa* is understanding the soul of Bakenyi survival. It is an ecology of movement, proving that sometimes, stability is found in drifting together.
