A language is not merely a mechanism for swapping labels; it is an entire philosophy, a repository of specialized local knowledge accumulated over centuries. For the **Bakenyi**, this repository is encapsulated in **Lukenye**.

As a minority group living among more populous communities, the Bakenyi have historically been bilingual, adapting fluidly to survive. However, this adaptability has led to a critical threshold where Lukenye is rarely spoken by children under the age of twelve.

### Why Lukenye Matters
Unlike neighboring Bantu languages, Lukenye developed specific terminologies related to water navigation, micro-climates of marshlands, and the behavioral biology of freshwater species. When a word like *Ebiswa* is replaced by a generic regional equivalent, we do not just lose a phonetic construct; we lose our specific ecological understanding of how floating marshlands drift with the winds.

### Our Strategy for Revival
To counter this linguistic decay, the Heritage Platform is initiating a community-driven audio dictionary. By pairing children with elders equipped with portable recorders, we are cataloging not just nouns, but the complex proverbs and songs that make our heritage unique.
